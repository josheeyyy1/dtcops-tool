# Scoping Call Script (30 minutes, free)

The job of this call is not to sell the audit. It's to diagnose out loud so
they experience what working with you is like. The audit sells itself when
they hear their own answers.

Have open: this script, `../audit/scoping-call-questions.md`, and their
site/store. Record the call (ask first).

## Minute 0–3: open

> "Thanks for making time. Here's how I'd like to use the 30 minutes: I'll
> ask how stock actually moves through your business, from placing a PO to
> a customer order shipping. By the end I'll tell you honestly whether I
> think there's money leaking and where. If your setup's solid, I'll say
> that. Sound alright?"

Then one calibration question:

> "Before I dig in: what made you take the call?"

Shut up and listen. Whatever they say is the pain they'll pay to fix; write
it down word for word. You'll use their phrasing later.

## Minute 3–22: diagnose

Run the short-call subset from the audit question set, in this order:
**Q2** (stock right now, where), **Q4** (how reorders happen), **Q6**
(regional balance), **Q8** (manual hours), **Q3** (last stock-out and cost).
Follow the pain, not the script order. Rules:

- When they give a bad answer, don't teach. Say "that's common" and move on.
  The report corrects; the call collects.
- Get numbers: hours, days out of stock, units, weeks of cover. Numbers on
  the call become the audit's cost table.
- If they ask "how would you fix that?" give the shape, not the blueprint:
  "That's a reorder-point problem. Velocity times lead time plus a safety
  buffer, per SKU, per warehouse. The work is in your data, which is what
  the audit is for."

## Minute 22–27: the handoff to the paid audit

Only if the call surfaced real pain. Summarise in their words:

> "So, playing back what you've told me: {{their phrase for the pain}},
> roughly {{N}} hours a week going into moving numbers around, and
> {{stock-out or over-stock example}}. In my experience those are symptoms
> of the same thing: no single source of truth and no reorder logic across
> your warehouses."

Then the offer, flat:

> "The next step, if you want it, is the Stock Audit. Two weeks. I get
> read-only access to Shopify and your 3PL reports, score eight areas of
> your stock operation, and hand you a report with a prioritised fix list
> and what each problem costs you per year, in your numbers. It's
> {{PRICE-AUDIT}}, and it comes off the build price if you later want me to
> fix what it finds. The report's yours either way, and plenty of people
> could action it without me."

Then stop talking. Silence is the close.

## Objections

- **"Can you just quote me for the build?"** "I could, but I'd be guessing
  and you'd be paying for my guess. The audit means the build quote is
  fixed-scope with no surprises. That's better for both of us."
- **"We can do the audit ourselves."** "You can, honestly. Touch 4 of my
  emails is the checklist. What you're buying is that I've seen this exact
  problem from the inside and I put your numbers on it in two weeks instead
  of a quarter."
- **"Too expensive."** Don't discount. "Fair enough. Take the checklist and
  run it internally. If the leak turns out bigger than the audit, come back."
- **Not qualified after all** (single region, sub-£1m, ERP in place): say
  so, kindly, on the call. "Honestly, an audit wouldn't pay for itself for
  you yet. Here's the one thing I'd watch for: {{the trigger that changes
  it}}." Then ask who they know. An honest no is your best referral source.

## Minute 27–30: close either way

- Sold: "I'll send a one-page confirmation and an access checklist today.
  Which day suits for the walkthrough in two weeks?" **Book the walkthrough
  before hanging up.**
- Maybe: name the follow-up date on the call. "I'll drop you a line Thursday
  either way." Then do it.
- No: thank them, referral ask, pipeline note, 90-day list.

After every call, 5 minutes: fill in `pipeline.csv` and write down the one
question that made them lean in. That question is your next cold email.
